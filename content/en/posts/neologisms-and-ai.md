Today while using Gemini I came across a neologism. I'm usually familiar with neologisms because
I'm relatively young and I deal with technology, the internet, and the language of the internet — English.
But this neologism I had never heard before: "pontiato".

When I asked Gemini for an explanation, it told me it comes from the English word "bridged", a term I recognize in English
but would have never translated as "pontiato".
And so I wonder: has Gemini, an AI, just coined a new term? Should I call the Accademia della Crusca?

I think this opens up a debate. Language used to be spoken by people; now it seems to be spoken by AI too.
A user could talk to Gemini and start casually using its neologism.
Maybe a newspaper decides to have an AI as an editor, and it becomes a linguistic authority in a media
that was once predominantly human.

About the term itself, I think Gemini is a victim of the fact that the main language it knows is English, and in
various optimizations, Italian was neglected.
I've also noticed that sometimes it says "como" instead of "come" — I'm talking about Gemini 3.5 flash. "Como" is Spanish.
Alternatively, it might have been trained on incorrect data, but that would seem strange.

In conclusion, I think LLMs will be an important change for minority languages like Italian.
On one hand, it should help Italian speakers translate into other languages; on the other hand, Italian is
a linguistically unimportant capability for these models.
No offense to Italians.
